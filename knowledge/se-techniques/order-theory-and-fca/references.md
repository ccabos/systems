# References

## On lattice and order theory

- Davey, B.A. & Priestley, H.A. (2002). *Introduction to Lattices
  and Order* (2nd ed.). Cambridge University Press. The standard
  textbook; covers posets, Galois connections, and complete
  lattices at the level the technique uses.
- Birkhoff, G. (1967). *Lattice Theory* (3rd ed.). AMS Colloquium
  Publications. The classical reference; deeper than needed for
  most applications but the original treatment of many constructs.
- Grätzer, G. (2011). *Lattice Theory: Foundation*. Birkhäuser.
  Modern treatment with complete proofs.

## On Formal Concept Analysis

- Wille, R. (1982). "Restructuring Lattice Theory: An Approach
  Based on Hierarchies of Concepts." In Rival, I. (ed.), *Ordered
  Sets*, 445–470. Reidel. The original paper introducing FCA.
- Ganter, B. & Wille, R. (1999). *Formal Concept Analysis:
  Mathematical Foundations*. Springer. The standard reference;
  covers the concept-lattice construction, the Duquenne–Guigues
  basis, and many-valued contexts (scaling).
- Carpineto, C. & Romano, G. (2004). *Concept Data Analysis:
  Theory and Applications*. Wiley. Practical treatment with worked
  examples.

## On FCA in the social and organisational sciences

- Priss, U. (2006). "Formal Concept Analysis in Information
  Science." *Annual Review of Information Science and Technology*,
  40, 521–543. Survey of FCA applications outside computer
  science, including organisational analysis.
- Wolff, K.E. (1994). "A First Course in Formal Concept Analysis."
  In Faulbaum, F. (ed.), *Advances in Statistical Software 4*,
  429–438. Gustav Fischer. Pedagogical introduction with
  social-science examples.
- Freeman, L.C. & White, D.R. (1993). "Using Galois Lattices to
  Represent Network Data." *Sociological Methodology*, 23,
  127–146. The first systematic use of FCA's concept lattice in
  social network analysis.

## On Galois connections in systems analysis

- Erné, M., Koslowski, J., Melton, A. & Strecker, G.E. (1993). "A
  Primer on Galois Connections." *Annals of the New York Academy of
  Sciences*, 704(1), 103–125. Compact introduction with
  systems-science examples.
- Spivak, D.I. (2014). *Category Theory for the Sciences.* MIT
  Press. Treats Galois connections as adjoint functors between
  preorder categories — the framing that connects this technique
  to category theory.

## On the systems-engineering hosts

- Leveson, N.G. (2012). *Engineering a Safer World: Systems
  Thinking Applied to Safety.* MIT Press. The STPA reference; the
  control-structure objects on which Application 3 operates.
- Pohl, K., Böckle, G. & van der Linden, F. (2005). *Software
  Product Line Engineering: Foundations, Principles, and
  Techniques.* Springer. The PLE reference; the variants × features
  matrices on which Application 5 operates.

## Relation to this project

The technique is the qualitative-mathematical complement to
`../linear-algebra/`. Both operate on the same project artefacts;
they answer different kinds of question. Order theory and FCA are
the right tools when the question is *about structure* — what is
the platform, where are the orphans, who has joint authority. They
are the wrong tools when the question is genuinely about magnitudes
or rates, which the project's qualitative artefacts cannot supply
in any case.
