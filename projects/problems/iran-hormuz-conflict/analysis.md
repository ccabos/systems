### Key structural changes since original analysis

1. **Supreme Leader changed.** Mojtaba Khamenei (no Grand Ayatollah
   status) installed under IRGC pressure. IRGC is now the dominant
   principal in the Iranian control structure. Any settlement must
   satisfy IRGC institutional interests.
2. **China did not fire its economic lever.** The "minimum viable
   intervention" recommended in Section 6.3 did not occur. China
   maintained oil purchases and indirect support. The US responded by
   sanctioning a major Chinese refinery. China–US economic tension is
   now a factor in the system.
3. **Pakistan is the active mediator.** Qatar and Oman are secondary;
   Pakistan successfully brokered the April ceasefire and is hosting
   talks. Pakistan is now a key controller in the peace process.
4. **UAE exited OPEC.** Gulf economic architecture is shifting. UAE is
   acting independently of Saudi Arabia and OPEC frameworks.
5. **Dual blockade created a symmetric coercion trap.** The US naval
   blockade of Iranian ports (from 13 April) mirrors Iran's Hormuz
   closure. Both sides are now applying rung-0 coercion simultaneously,
   with no face-saving exit available to either without appearing to
   concede first.
6. **Core negotiating deadlock is explicit.** Iran: end the war first,
   nuclear talks later. US: nuclear settlement and unconditional Hormuz
   reopening before sanctions lifted. These are mirror-image sequencing
   demands; neither side can move first without appearing to capitulate.

## STPA Step 1 — Define Purpose of the Analysis

### 1.1 Losses

Losses are unacceptable outcomes the stakeholders want to avoid, stated
in stakeholder language.

| ID | Loss |
|----|------|
| L1 | Full-scale regional war — direct military exchange between Iran and Israel and/or the United States that produces mass casualties, physical destruction of critical infrastructure, and potentially nuclear escalation |
| L2 | Iranian nuclear weapons acquisition without international verification and constraint |
| L3 | Sustained closure or effective interdiction of the Strait of Hormuz, triggering a global energy crisis and severe economic damage to oil-dependent states |
| L4 | Regional nuclear proliferation cascade — Saudi Arabia, Turkey, or Egypt acquiring nuclear weapons in response to L2 |
| L5 | Iranian regime collapse leading to a power vacuum, loss of custody of nuclear materials, and ungoverned proxy networks |
| L6 | Permanent entrenchment of Iranian proxy warfare — Hezbollah, Houthis, Iraqi militias — as the normal operating mode of the region, preventing any durable political settlement |
| L7 | Loss of freedom of navigation in the Red Sea and Arabian Sea approaches to Hormuz, forcing permanent re-routing of global shipping at prohibitive cost |

### 1.2 Hazards

Hazards are system states that, in a worst-case environment, lead to a
loss. They are conditions of the system, not events.

| ID | Hazard | Leads to |
|----|--------|----------|
| H1 | Iran holds a nuclear weapon or is within days of assembly, without a verified constraint agreement in place | L1, L2, L4 |
| H2 | Direct military exchanges between Iran and Israel occur without an active deconfliction channel or ceasefire mechanism | L1, L5 |
| H3 | Iran or its proxies hold credible, ready capability to close or substantially interdict Hormuz, and no multilateral framework constrains its use | L3, L7 |
| H4 | Iran's proxy network is simultaneously activated and receiving Iranian supply without a political process that offers an off-ramp | L1, L6 |
| H5 | The US and Iran have no functioning diplomatic channel and each misreads the other's military moves as preparation for a first strike | L1 |
| H6 | The UNSC is paralysed and no alternative multilateral governance mechanism exists to manage the crisis | L1, L2, L3 |
| H7 | Gulf states have no mutual non-aggression framework with Iran and cannot separately negotiate security guarantees | L3, L6 |

### 1.3 System-Level Constraints

One constraint per hazard, stated as a must-condition.

| ID | Constraint | Addresses |
|----|-----------|-----------|
| SC1 | Iran must not hold assembled nuclear weapons or unrestricted weapons-grade fissile material without a verified international agreement governing its disposition | H1 |
| SC2 | Iran and Israel must maintain at minimum one active deconfliction channel at all times; any direct military exchange must be bounded by a pre-agreed escalation ceiling | H2 |
| SC3 | Freedom of commercial navigation through the Strait of Hormuz must be guaranteed by a multilateral framework that Iran has formally endorsed and that gives Iran a positive economic stake in compliance | H3 |
| SC4 | Iran's proxy supply chains must be subject to at least one active political process that offers the proxies a legitimate political alternative to indefinite warfare | H4 |
| SC5 | The US and Iran must maintain at minimum one indirect communication channel (back-channel) capable of transmitting authoritative signals during a military crisis | H5 |
| SC6 | A great-power coordination mechanism outside the UNSC must exist, capable of communicating and enforcing red lines among the US, Russia, and China on Iran | H6 |
| SC7 | The Gulf Cooperation Council states must have a formal security framework with Iran that separates their bilateral relations from the Iran–Israel–US axis of conflict | H7 |

## STPA Step 4 — Loss Scenarios

Step 4 asks *why* each major UCA could occur. Causes are organised
around the five control-loop failure modes: (PM) wrong process model ·
(FB) missing or corrupted feedback · (AC) actuator failure · (CP)
controlled process changed state unexpectedly · (ED) external
disturbance.

Each scenario closes with a **remedy pointer** — the structural change
that would break the causal chain. Remedies are elaborated in Section 5.

---

### Scenario S1 — US sanctions fail to constrain Iranian enrichment (→ UCA-1, UCA-2, UCA-3 → H1)

**Causal chain:**

- **(PM)** The US process model holds that maximum economic pressure
  will force a rational cost-benefit calculation in Tehran. This model
  was approximately valid when Iranian reformist factions held
  significant influence (pre-2018). After the JCPOA collapse and the
  systematic elimination of reformist political space, the effective
  decision-maker is the Supreme Leader and the IRGC, who treat
  economic pain as confirming the necessity of strategic autonomy
  (nuclear deterrent). The US process model has not been updated.
- **(FB)** The feedback signal Iran actually sends — accelerated
  enrichment, reduced IAEA access, proxy escalation — is read by the
  US as evidence that more pressure is needed, not as evidence that
  the tool is counterproductive. This is a self-sealing rung-1 process
  model on the US side: "if it's not working, we haven't done enough."
- **(ED)** China's discount oil purchases insulate Iran from
  approximately 30–40 % of the economic impact, making the sanctions
  instrument structurally weaker than the US process model assumes.

**Remedy pointer:** Replace the sanctions-as-compellence model with a
sequenced-relief-for-verified-steps model. Requires accepting that
China's participation is necessary, not optional. → Option A, Option E.

---

### Scenario S2 — Iran–Israel direct exchange escalates without circuit breaker (→ UCA-5, UCA-19, UCA-20 → H2)

**Causal chain:**

- **(PM)** Israel's process model treats each Iranian missile salvo as
  a compellence act that requires visible retaliation to restore
  deterrence. Iran's process model treats each Israeli strike as
  confirmation of existential threat, justifying escalation. Both
  process models are individually consistent; together they form a
  positive feedback loop with no stable equilibrium short of full war
  or a mutual exhaustion point.
- **(FB)** There is no established direct Iran–Israel communication
  channel. Deconfliction signals must route through the US, Qatar, or
  Oman — each of which adds latency and interpretive noise. During a
  fast-moving exchange, the feedback arrives too slowly to interrupt
  the escalation ladder.
- **(AC)** The US "amber light" moderating signal to Israel
  (UCA-20) depends on Israel notifying the US before a strike. In a
  crisis, Israel's decision loop may be faster than the notification
  protocol.
- **(ED)** Domestic political pressures in both Israel and Iran reward
  leaders for appearing strong under attack; the political cost of
  restraint exceeds the strategic benefit in the short term.

**Remedy pointer:** Establish a standing Iran–Israel–US crisis
communication channel before the next exchange, not during it.
Require mandatory pre-notification windows. → Option B, Option E.

---

### Scenario S3 — Hormuz closure triggered by an Iranian reactive decision (→ UCA-11, UCA-12 → H3)

**Causal chain:**

- **(PM)** Iran's IRGC Naval process model holds that Hormuz closure
  is a proportionate response to a major strike on Iranian territory,
  regardless of the economic self-damage. This is a rung-1 identity
  logic ("we will burn the house down rather than submit") rather than
  a rung-3 cost-benefit calculation.
- **(FB)** Iran receives no real-time signal from China quantifying
  the damage a Hormuz closure would cause to Chinese economic
  interests. China has not communicated a clear red line to Iran on
  this question. Without that signal, the IRGC's process model
  underweights Chinese backlash.
- **(CP)** A Hormuz closure would be self-defeating — it would cut
  Iran's own oil revenue and sever the Chinese lifeline — but the
  controlled process (Hormuz shipping) could change state (closed)
  faster than Iran's economic feedback loop registers the damage. The
  closure could occur before the self-harm is felt.

**Remedy pointer:** China must communicate a credible red line to Iran:
Hormuz closure = end of Chinese economic support. This converts
Iran's rung-1 reactive logic into a rung-0 constraint (economic
survival). Simultaneously, a multilateral Hormuz framework gives Iran
a positive economic stake in keeping it open (transit fees, market
access). → Option C.

---

### Scenario S4 — Proxy network activates autonomously beyond Iranian control (→ UCA-10 → H4)

**Causal chain:**

- **(PM)** Iran's process model assumes the proxy network is a
  controllable instrument — that de-escalation orders will be obeyed
  as readily as escalation orders. Historical evidence is mixed:
  Hezbollah, after decades of deep integration with the IRGC, has
  shown high compliance; the Houthis, with weaker institutional ties
  and a strong independent political identity, have shown growing
  autonomy. Post-2024 Hezbollah leadership degradation may have
  weakened the command channel further.
- **(FB)** Iran receives battlefield reports from proxies but limited
  honest feedback on proxy political calculus. Proxies have an
  incentive to overstate their controllability to maintain the supply
  relationship.
- **(AC)** The "stop" control action reaches Houthi leadership
  through IRGC liaisons. If those liaisons are degraded (killed,
  communications cut), the actuator fails.

**Remedy pointer:** Negotiate directly with each proxy's political
leadership on a political settlement that gives them a legitimate
stake (Yemen peace deal for Houthis; Lebanese state-building deal
that preserves Hezbollah's political role without military autonomy).
Removes the autonomous-operation incentive. → Option D.

---

### Scenario S5 — IAEA verification collapses, leaving adversaries without a reliable weapons-threshold signal (→ UCA-15, UCA-17 → H1)

**Causal chain:**

- **(PM)** Iran's process model holds that full IAEA access is a
  concession that only makes sense after sanctions relief, not before.
  The US process model holds that sanctions relief can only be
  justified after verification. Each side's sequencing logic is
  internally consistent; together they produce a deadlock in which
  neither step is taken first.
- **(FB)** Without IAEA access, the US and Israel must rely on
  national intelligence assessments of Iranian enrichment levels.
  These are less accurate and less timely than IAEA monitoring.
  The information vacuum induces worst-case planning on the Israeli
  side, shortening the perceived window for a preventive strike.
- **(R2)** IAEA formal demands arrive as rung-2 treaty obligations
  (NPT, safeguards agreements). Iran's rung-1 sovereignty frame
  rejects them as illegitimate pressure, and the rejection is made
  publicly, which makes compliance politically costly.

**Remedy pointer:** Decouple IAEA access from sanctions sequencing.
Offer Iran face-saving technical arrangements (non-IAEA third-party
monitoring as an interim step; partial access before full sanctions
relief). → Option A.

---

### Scenario S6 — Great-power veto paralysis blocks every multilateral off-ramp (→ UCA-24 → H6)

**Causal chain:**

- **(PM)** Russia's process model: a managed, below-war-threshold
  Iran crisis drains US resources and attention from Europe and the
  Indo-Pacific. Resolution is against Russian interests. Russia
  therefore has an active incentive to block UNSC resolutions without
  appearing to endorse Iranian behaviour.
- **(PM)** China's process model is more ambivalent: a full regional
  war disrupts Chinese oil imports and destabilises BRI investment.
  China wants the crisis managed below a war threshold but is
  reluctant to exercise its Iran leverage visibly, fearing precedent
  for US demands on Taiwan.
- **(CP)** The UNSC's veto structure means the controlled process
  (Iran's behaviour) cannot be changed through the UNSC channel while
  Russia or China dissents. This is a structural feature, not a
  temporary failure.

**Remedy pointer:** Create a US–China–Russia crisis coordination
channel outside the UNSC — modelled on the Cold War hotline and the
P5+1 nuclear process — that allows red-line communication without
requiring public UNSC votes. China's economic leverage over Iran is
deployed privately, not through UN resolutions. → Option E.

## Section 5 — Remedy Catalogue

Each option is a structural intervention derived from the Step 4
scenarios. Options are not mutually exclusive; the sequencing
recommendation in Section 6 shows how they interact.

For each option the table shows: the UCAs it addresses · the
constraints it must satisfy (`problem.md §Constraints`) · the rung
uplift it provides · tractability (political difficulty) · time
horizon to first effect.

### Option A — Sequenced Nuclear Verification Deal (JCPOA-2)

**What it changes in the control structure:**  
Fixes the process-model deadlock in the US–Iran nuclear loop
(S5). Replaces the current rung-0 sanctions lever (which does
not reach Iran's rung-1 decision-makers on nuclear matters) with a
rung-2/3 verified-compliance channel that gives both sides a
self-enforcing incentive.

**Core elements:**

1. **Interim monitoring arrangement** — Iran grants IAEA or a
   jointly-agreed third-party monitor (e.g. Swiss Federal Nuclear
   Safety Inspectorate) read-only access to enrichment data before
   any sanctions relief. Breaks the sequencing deadlock without
   requiring either side to move first unconditionally.
2. **Verified enrichment cap** — Iran commits to a specified maximum
   enrichment level (e.g. 20 % U-235, with 60 %+ stockpiles
   diluted under IAEA supervision). Cap is technically monitored,
   not politically asserted.
3. **Staged sanctions relief** — each verified step triggers a
   pre-agreed sanctions waiver. Removes the "US can walk away at
   any time" fragility of JCPOA-1 by embedding relief in an
   international legal instrument with European co-signatories and
   explicit Chinese and Russian endorsement.
4. **Sunset-clause replacement** — unlike JCPOA-1, enrichment caps
   are permanent, not time-limited, in exchange for a permanent
   civilian nuclear fuel supply guarantee (Russia/China as guarantors).

**UCAs addressed:** UCA-1, UCA-2, UCA-3, UCA-14, UCA-15, UCA-16,
UCA-17, UCA-18.

**Constraints:**  
- Requires US Senate engagement (legislative sanctions).  
- Requires Supreme Leader sign-off; cannot be delivered by any
  Iranian president alone.  
- Israel must be persuaded that the verified cap genuinely extends
  the breakout timeline; Israeli intelligence assessment is a
  de facto veto on US political will.

**Rung uplift:** US–Iran nuclear loop: rung 0 → rung 2–3.
IAEA–Iran loop: rung 1 → rung 2–3.

**Tractability:** Difficult. Requires simultaneous domestic political
movement in both the US and Iran.

**Time horizon:** 18–36 months to first effect after talks open.

### Option B — Iran–Israel–Gulf Regional Security Architecture

**What it changes in the control structure:**  
Fixes the missing deconfliction channel between Iran and Israel
(S2) and creates a rung-2 formal framework between Iran and Gulf
states (H7). Adds a structural off-ramp that does not exist today.

**Core elements:**

1. **Standing crisis communication channel** — Qatar or Oman hosts
   a permanent three-way back-channel: Iran, Israel (via a
   non-public intermediary — Jordan has played this role), US.
   Pre-agreed message protocols for: "we are about to strike,"
   "we are retaliating," "we propose a pause." This is not a
   peace treaty; it is a circuit breaker.
2. **Iran–GCC non-aggression framework** — builds on the 2023
   Saudi–Iran normalisation. Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Kuwait sign a
   formal non-aggression pact with Iran, separating their bilateral
   security from the Iran–Israel–US conflict axis. Gives Iran a
   regional political win without conceding on the nuclear issue.
3. **Mutual restraint on proxy-origin attacks** — Iran commits (in
   a side agreement) to not directing proxy attacks on GCC
   infrastructure; GCC commits to not hosting offensive military
   operations against Iran. Verified by Qatar mediation.

**UCAs addressed:** UCA-4, UCA-5, UCA-7, UCA-19, UCA-20, UCA-25,
UCA-26.

**Constraints:**  
- Israel will not sign directly with Iran; must operate through
  intermediaries.  
- Iran will not accept a framework that constrains proxy operations
  unless there is a political concession (e.g. Gaza reconstruction
  pathway, Lebanese state-building investment).  
- GCC states will not sign unless the US security guarantee is
  maintained.

**Rung uplift:** Iran–Israel loop: rung 0 → rung 1–2 (crisis
channel). Iran–GCC loop: rung 1 → rung 2.

**Tractability:** Moderate. The 2023 Saudi–Iran normalisation is
a proof of concept. The crisis-channel component requires only
quiet agreement among three parties; it does not need to be public.

**Time horizon:** 6–12 months for crisis channel; 12–24 months for
the GCC framework.

### Option C — Multilateral Hormuz Freedom-of-Navigation Framework

**What it changes in the control structure:**  
Converts the Hormuz closure threat from a rung-1 identity-driven
lever (which Iran cannot credibly bind itself not to use) into a
rung-0 economic constraint (which Iran structurally cannot afford
to trigger) by making China an active enforcer rather than a
passive bystander.

**Core elements:**

1. **China as co-guarantor** — China publicly and privately
   communicates to Iran that a Hormuz closure will end Chinese oil
   purchases and economic support. This converts China's tacit
   moderation into an explicit control action. It is the single most
   powerful single intervention available.
2. **Hormuz Transit Authority (multilateral)** — Iran, Oman, the
   GCC, India, China, Japan, and South Korea sign a freedom-of-
   navigation protocol, with Iran receiving a nominal transit-fee
   revenue stream (rung-0 economic incentive) for each tanker
   passing without incident. Gives Iran a positive financial stake
   in keeping the strait open.
3. **Houthi Red Sea component** — the framework explicitly includes
   the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden approaches. A Yemen peace deal (see
   Option D) is the prerequisite; without it, Houthi attacks
   undercut the protocol.
4. **Multinational escort coordination** — existing Combined
   Maritime Forces (CMF) operations formalized as a standing Hormuz
   escort arrangement, with China and India participating (giving
   Iran less grounds to frame it as a US containment operation).

**UCAs addressed:** UCA-8, UCA-11, UCA-12, UCA-13.

**Constraints:**  
- China must be willing to use its Iran leverage publicly, which
  creates a precedent it resists.  
- Iran will only sign if the framework is framed as an equal-partner
  agreement, not a US-imposed constraint.  
- The Houthi component requires the Yemen peace deal to be
  advanced first.

**Rung uplift:** Iran–Hormuz control loop: rung 1 → rung 0–2
(economic constraint + treaty obligation). China–Iran loop: rung 0
(implicit) → rung 0 (explicit) + rung 2 (treaty).

**Tractability:** Moderate-to-difficult. The China pivot is the
key; without it, the framework is a paper agreement Iran can
exit at will.

**Time horizon:** 12–18 months once China commits.

### Option D — Proxy De-escalation: Targeted Political Settlements

**What it changes in the control structure:**  
Addresses the autonomous-proxy problem (S4) and UCA-9 by giving
each proxy a legitimate political pathway that is more valuable
than indefinite warfare, removing the indefinite-supply incentive
from Iran and the autonomous-operation incentive from the proxies.

**Sub-options by proxy:**

**D1 — Yemen / Houthis**  
A UN-supervised Yemen peace process that gives the Houthi movement
political recognition in the Yemeni governing structure (as a
party, not a military force), Saudi investment in northern Yemen
reconstruction, and Iranian withdrawal of advanced missile/drone
supply. The Houthis' leverage is the Red Sea; their demand is
political legitimacy. Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 needs regional
stability. The interests are alignable.

**D2 — Lebanon / Hezbollah**  
A Lebanon state-reconstruction compact — IMF/World Bank investment
conditional on Lebanese Armed Forces assuming sole authority south
of the Litani River. Hezbollah retains political party status but
loses the military-wing justification for independent arms. This
does not eliminate Hezbollah; it changes its operating rung from
rung-0 (armed force) to rung-1/2 (political party under
constitutional framework).

**D3 — Iraq / Shia militias**  
An Iraqi sovereignty compact: US agrees to a timetabled drawdown
of combat forces; Iraq formally integrates Popular Mobilisation
Forces into the Iraqi Army chain of command; Iran accepts reduced
operational control over Iraqi-based proxies in exchange for
US force reduction. Leverages both sides' stated positions.

**D4 — Gaza / Hamas**  
A Gaza reconstruction and governance pathway: Hamas transitions
from governing authority to political party under a Palestinian
Authority or UN interim administration, with reconstruction
financing conditional on disarmament. This is the hardest sub-
option but the one most directly under current international
pressure.

**UCAs addressed:** UCA-7, UCA-9, UCA-10, UCA-21.

**Constraints:**  
- Each proxy settlement is independent but interconnected: Iran
  will not agree to D1 while under maximum military pressure on D4.  
- Option D does not resolve the nuclear question and should not be
  used as a substitute for Option A.

**Rung uplift:** Proxy control loops: rung 0 → rung 1–2 per sub-
option. Houthi–Hormuz interface: rung 0 → rung 2 (political party
under peace agreement).

**Tractability:** Variable. D3 (Iraq) is the most immediately
tractable — both the US and Iraq have stated interests aligned.
D2 (Lebanon) became more tractable after Hezbollah's 2024
leadership degradation. D1 (Yemen) requires Saudi political will.
D4 (Gaza) is the most contested.

**Time horizon:** D3 12 months; D2 18–24 months; D1 18–36 months;
D4 36+ months.

### Option E — Great-Power Crisis Coordination Mechanism

**What it changes in the control structure:**  
Fixes the UNSC-paralysis problem (S6) by creating a working
coordination channel between the US, China, and Russia that
operates at rung 0–2 (direct interest bargaining + agreed
protocols) rather than requiring rung-6 multilateral legitimacy
through the UNSC.

**Core elements:**

1. **US–China Middle East crisis hotline** — modelled on the
   US–Soviet Moscow–Washington hotline. Specific to the Middle East /
   Iran portfolio. Mandatory activation within 2 hours of any
   direct Iran–Israel or US–Iran military exchange. China uses the
   channel to communicate its Iran pressure actions; the US uses it
   to communicate its strike plans.
2. **Russia as Iran behaviour guarantor** — Russia accepts a
   designated role as the party responsible for ensuring Iran does
   not conduct first-strike nuclear use or Hormuz closure during a
   crisis. In exchange, the US accepts a specific set of Russian
   interests (e.g. no NATO expansion to former Soviet states that
   share a border with Russia as a linked concession). This is
   realpolitik bargaining, not normative agreement.
3. **Trilateral red-line protocol** — US, China, and Russia agree
   privately on three shared red lines: (a) no first use of nuclear
   weapons; (b) no Hormuz closure; (c) no Israeli or US strike on
   Iranian civilian nuclear infrastructure without a 72-hour notice
   window. None of these require UNSC endorsement.

**UCAs addressed:** UCA-22, UCA-23, UCA-24; provides the
institutional backbone that makes Options A, B, and C durable.

**Constraints:**  
- Russia's willingness to participate is contingent on receiving
  something of value; this requires the US to make a concession in
  a domain Russia values, which is politically costly domestically.  
- China's willingness to use its Iran leverage visibly sets a
  precedent it fears will be applied to Taiwan. Requires careful
  scope limitation.

**Rung uplift:** US–China–Russia on Iran: rung 0 (implicit power
competition) → rung 0–2 (explicit bargained protocol). UNSC
bypass removes the rung-0 veto paralysis.

**Tractability:** Difficult but not unprecedented. The P5+1 nuclear
talks and the post-Cold War OSCE architecture show that adversarial
great powers can operate standing coordination mechanisms when the
alternative is escalation risk to all parties.

**Time horizon:** The hotline component can be stood up in 3–6
months if political will exists; the full protocol requires
18–24 months of negotiation.

## Section 6 — Sequencing Recommendation

The five options are not independent: some must precede others, some
can run in parallel, and some block each other if pursued in the
wrong order. The sequencing logic below follows the STAMP principle:
fix the control loops that are prerequisites for all other loops
first.

### 6.1 Dependency map

```
Option E (great-power channel)
    ├──► Option A (nuclear deal) — requires US–China coordination on
    │                              sanctions relief and verification
    ├──► Option B (regional architecture) — requires US–Iran channel
    │                                       that E enables
    └──► Option C (Hormuz framework) — requires China to use its
                                        Iran leverage, which E makes
                                        explicit

Option D (proxy settlements) — partially independent; D3 (Iraq) can
    start immediately; D1 and D2 are prerequisites for Option C's
    Houthi/Red Sea component
```

### 6.2 Recommended sequence

| Phase | Timeframe | Action | Prerequisite |
|-------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| 0 | Immediately | Open or reconnect the Oman back-channel between the US and Iran to prevent a crisis communication blackout | None |
| 0 | Immediately | Private US–China communication establishing that China will activate its Hormuz red line in a crisis | None |
| 1 | 0–6 months | Option E: Stand up US–China Middle East crisis hotline | Phase 0 |
| 1 | 0–6 months | Option D3: US–Iraq sovereignty compact and militia drawdown | None |
| 2 | 6–12 months | Option B: Iran–Israel–Gulf crisis channel via Qatar/Oman | Phase 1 |
| 2 | 6–18 months | Option D2: Lebanon reconstruction compact / Hezbollah political normalisation | Phase 1 |
| 2 | 6–18 months | Option D1: Yemen peace process relaunch | Phase 1 |
| 3 | 12–24 months | Option C: Multilateral Hormuz framework | D1 and D2 advanced; Phase 1 E established |
| 3 | 12–24 months | Option A: Interim nuclear monitoring arrangement | Phase 1 E; Phase 2 B (reduces Israeli veto pressure) |
| 4 | 24–48 months | Option A: Full JCPOA-2 agreement | Option C signed; D4 Gaza pathway opened |
| 4 | 36+ months | Option D4: Gaza governance pathway | All other options in place |

### 6.3 Minimum viable intervention

If only one intervention is possible in the immediate term, the
analysis identifies it as:

> **Private US–China communication that China will close Iran's
> economic lifeline if Hormuz is interdicted, delivered before the
> next Iran–Israel military exchange.**

This single action:
- Converts Iran's most dangerous rung-1 (reactive closure) threat
  into a rung-0 constraint (economic survival) without requiring
  any public agreement.
- Does not require Iranian consent or US–Iran direct talks.
- Is achievable within the current geopolitical configuration.
- Has a time horizon of weeks, not months.

It does not solve any of the structural problems — but it removes
the single most catastrophic near-term loss (L3, Hormuz closure)
from the near-term risk set, buying time for the longer-horizon
options to operate.

## Section 7 — Summary: Structural Diagnosis

The central finding of this analysis is that the Iran regional
conflict persists not because any single actor is irrational, but
because the control structure has a set of deeply-embedded
architectural failures that reproduce conflict regardless of who
occupies each role.

| Architectural failure | Manifestation | Fix |
|----------------------|--------------|-----|
| Rung-0 lever (sanctions) targeting rung-1 controller (Supreme Leader) | 25 years of sanctions have not changed Iranian nuclear trajectory | Replace compellence with sequenced-verification-for-relief (Option A) |
| No circuit-breaker channel between Iran and Israel | Every exchange risks uncontrolled escalation | Standing crisis channel (Option B) |
| China's Iran leverage is unused because it is not explicitly invoked | Iran does not internalise the cost of Hormuz closure | China red-line communication (Option C, Phase 0) |
| Proxies have no legitimate political pathway, so warfare is rational | Proxy network self-sustains independently of Iranian intent | Targeted political settlements (Option D) |
| UNSC paralysis removes the only formal governance layer | No multilateral off-ramp when crisis peaks | Great-power coordination outside UNSC (Option E) |
| US process model (sanctions = compellence) is empirically falsified but not updated | Policy continues despite evidence of failure | Model update: China partnership is necessary, not optional (Options A + E) |

The conflict is solvable. The control structure can be changed. But
it requires accepting three propositions that are currently resisted
by at least one major actor:

1. **China must be a co-architect of the solution**, not a passive
   beneficiary. Its Iran leverage is the system's single most
   powerful unused control action.
2. **Sanctions relief must precede, or at minimum accompany,
   verifiable Iranian steps** — the current sequencing deadlock
   produces L2, not SC1.
3. **Each proxy conflict requires a political settlement, not just
   a military solution** — the 2024 Hezbollah degradation, the
   ongoing Yemen war, and the Gaza conflict all demonstrate that
   military pressure without a political pathway produces
   reconstitution, not resolution.

## Section 8 — Active-War Addendum **[UPDATED 29 April 2026]**

Sections 1–7 were written as a pre-war crisis analysis. This section
updates the remedy catalogue and sequencing for the active-war
situation as of 29 April 2026.

### 8.1 What the pre-war analysis got right

- The rung-1 / rung-0 mismatch between US sanctions and Iranian
  decision-making was correctly identified; the war demonstrated
  that Iran's leadership chose escalation over economic survival.
- UCA-12 (Iran closes Hormuz despite Chinese interests) was correctly
  identified as a live risk; it was triggered on 28 February 2026.
- UCA-19 (Israel strikes before a diplomatic ultimatum) was correctly
  identified; it occurred.
- The UNSC paralysis scenario (S6) was correctly predicted; the
  ceasefire was achieved outside the UNSC by Pakistan, not through
  the Security Council.
- L5 (regime collapse) was flagged as a hazard; it is partially
  realised with the IRGC-dominant Mojtaba Khamenei succession.

### 8.2 What the pre-war analysis missed or under-weighted

- **China did not apply its economic lever.** The minimum viable
  intervention (Section 6.3) assumed China's self-interest in
  Hormuz would translate into a visible red line to Iran. It did
  not. China chose strategic neutrality and indirect support over
  active mediation. The analysis over-estimated Chinese pragmatism
  relative to Chinese strategic interest in letting the US be
  consumed by the conflict.
- **Pakistan as mediator.** Pakistan was not modelled as a controller.
  It proved to be the only party capable of brokering the April 2026
  ceasefire, exploiting its relationships with both the US and Iran.
  Pakistan should be incorporated into any future control structure
  model as a Tier 2 mediator alongside Qatar and Oman.
- **IRGC as dominant successor controller.** The analysis modelled
  the Supreme Leader as the blocking constraint on Iranian
  flexibility. The war has shown that the IRGC is the actual
  constraint — and with a new, legitimacy-deficient Supreme Leader
  dependent on IRGC support, IRGC institutional interests are now
  the primary determinant of Iranian negotiating positions.
- **Dual-blockade symmetry.** The US responded to Hormuz closure
  with its own naval blockade of Iranian ports. This was not
  modelled. It creates a symmetric coercion trap: both sides are
  applying rung-0 coercion with no face-saving exit available
  without appearing to concede first.

### 8.3 Updated remedy priorities for the active-war phase

The five options from Section 5 remain structurally valid, but
their sequencing and content must be adapted for the active-war
context. The pre-war phase sequence is replaced by the following.

#### Immediate (within current ceasefire window, before late May 2026)

**Priority 1 — Break the sequencing deadlock via a bridging formula.**
The Islamabad Talks failed because both sides have mirror-image
sequencing demands (Iran: war ends first; US: nuclear settlement
first). The bridging formula that breaks this is: **simultaneous
partial steps** — Iran agrees to IAEA access and enrichment freeze;
the US agrees to a partial, reversible sanctions waiver; both steps
are taken on the same day. Pakistan and Oman are the natural hosts
for this agreement. Neither side "goes first."

This is a modification of Option A that addresses the sequencing
deadlock directly. It requires the US to abandon the "unconditional
Hormuz reopening first" position, accepting instead that partial
Hormuz reopening accompanies rather than precedes the nuclear step.

**Priority 2 — IRGC legitimacy acknowledgment.**
Mojtaba Khamenei's authority depends on IRGC backing. Any deal
that humiliates the IRGC institutionally will not survive. The US
must structure any agreement so that the IRGC can present it
internally as a strategic achievement, not a defeat. Framing the
nuclear freeze as "Iran's sovereign choice to demonstrate
responsibility" rather than "compliance under military pressure"
is a rung-1 face-saving requirement, not a substantive concession.

**Priority 3 — Separate the dual blockades procedurally.**
The symmetric blockade trap can be broken by treating the two
blockades as separate negotiations: Iran lifts Hormuz civilian
shipping restrictions; the US lifts the naval blockade of Iranian
ports. These are operationally independent actions that can be
sequenced within days of each other, giving both sides a visible
reciprocal gesture without either "going first" on the larger issues.

#### Near-term (within extended ceasefire, 1–3 months)

**Priority 4 — China engagement at the right rung.**
China chose neutrality in the pre-war phase; the war has now made
neutrality costly (Chinese oil supply disrupted, US sanctioning
Chinese refineries). China now has a positive incentive to engage
that it lacked before. The approach must change: rather than asking
China to apply its Iran lever (which China resists as precedent),
frame China's role as **co-author of a Hormuz multilateral
framework** (Option C) that is in China's direct interest. China
gets a formal stake in the Hormuz arrangement rather than being asked
to coerce a partner.

**Priority 5 — Lebanon and Yemen ceasefire consolidation.**
The Lebanon war must be frozen before any Iran-wide deal is possible.
A Lebanon ceasefire that preserves Hezbollah's political role without
its military autonomy (Option D2) is now more achievable than before
the war, given Hezbollah's further degradation. Yemen (D1) follows
the same logic: the Houthis' political demands (recognition, north
Yemen reconstruction) remain the leverage point.

#### Medium-term (post-ceasefire consolidation, 3–12 months)

**Priority 6 — Full nuclear deal (Option A, modified).**
Once the dual blockade is lifted and a Lebanon ceasefire is in place,
the conditions for a full nuclear agreement are significantly better
than at any previous point. The war has demonstrated that the military
option does not eliminate Iran's nuclear capability; a verified cap
is the only durable solution. The modified JCPOA-2 framework from
Option A applies, with the additional requirement that the IRGC's
institutional interests are structurally accommodated (e.g. a civilian
nuclear programme under IRGC-affiliated entities).

### 8.4 Updated minimum viable intervention

The original Section 6.3 minimum viable intervention (China private
red line on Hormuz) did not occur. Given the current state, the
updated minimum viable intervention is:

> **A procedural separation of the dual blockades: Iran suspends
> Hormuz civilian shipping restrictions; the US suspends its naval
> blockade of Iranian ports. Both actions taken within a 48-hour
> window, mediated by Pakistan. No linkage to the nuclear question
> in this step.**

This:
- Breaks the symmetric coercion trap without requiring either side
  to address the harder nuclear sequencing question.
- Provides immediate relief on L3 (energy crisis) and L7 (shipping).
- Is achievable within the current ceasefire window before late May.
- Gives Pakistan a concrete mediation success that reinforces its
  role as the key channel.
- Creates momentum for the nuclear and political talks without
  prejudging their outcome.
