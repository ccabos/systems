# Three Dangerous Rung Mismatches

The seven rungs admit many possible per-arrow combinations. Three
mismatch patterns recur across the catalogued social systems and
account for most of the rung-related failures.

## Pattern A — Asymmetric Loop (rung-1 down, rung-3+ up)

**Shape.** Control actions go downward at rung 1 (authority,
tradition, rhetoric). Feedback is *expected* to flow upward at rung
3 or higher (empirical reports, audited data, replicated findings).
The controller's process model rejects rung-3 feedback unless it
arrives wrapped in rung-1 packaging — which strips the empirical
content.

**Why it is dangerous.** The loop *appears* closed: arrows exist in
both directions. But the upward arrow is filtered through a
process model that does not admit the rung the feedback was
generated at. Errors in the controlled process accumulate
unobserved.

**Instances observed.**

- Doctrinal authorities receiving abuse reports that arrive as
  rung-3 evidence and are classified as rung-1 hostility.
- Autocracies receiving independent press reports as rung-1
  treason rather than rung-3 information.
- Family systems receiving children's developmental data as rung-1
  defiance rather than rung-3 health signals.
- Single-party leaderships receiving cadre dissent as rung-1
  disloyalty rather than rung-3 ground truth.

**Generic remedy — rung elevation of the feedback channel.**
Insert a new feedback channel that operates at rung 3, runs
independently of the rung-1 hierarchy, and reaches the apex of
control structure directly. The new channel does not replace the
rung-1 channel; it parallels it. Concrete instances:

- Mandatory external audit (financial, abuse, environmental).
- Independent ombudsperson with statutory access.
- Pre-registered studies whose results cannot be filtered by the
  hierarchy that funded them.
- Constitutional courts that can hear rung-3 evidence against
  rung-1 sovereign claims.

The remedy is structural, not cultural. It does not require the
rung-1 controller to accept rung-3 feedback; it bypasses the
controller's filter by routing the rung-3 channel to a separate
oversight body whose existence is independent of the controller.

## Pattern B — Claimed-Rung Inflation

**Shape.** The system publicly claims to operate at a high rung
(rung 6 deliberation, rung 3-4 evidence-based decision-making) but
actually operates at rung 1 or 0. Stakeholders experience the gap
empirically — they see decisions made on grounds the system claims
to disavow.

**Why it is dangerous.** Operating-rung inflation produces the
worst of both worlds: the system pays the costs of the lower rung
(arbitrary decisions, narrow reasoning, suppression of dissent)
while incurring the costs of the higher rung (legitimacy claims it
cannot back, expectations it cannot meet). Trust erodes at a rate
proportional to the claim-vs-reality gap.

**Instances observed.**

- Theocracies that claim divine guidance (rung 1, sanctified) but
  operate via clerical politics (rung 0/1).
- One-party states that claim "people's democracy" (rung 6) while
  operating through party discipline (rung 0/1).
- Universities that claim peer-reviewed knowledge (rung 4) while
  promoting through prestige networks (rung 1).
- Corporations that claim shareholder accountability (rung 3 by
  audited financials) while operating through CEO–board capture
  (rung 1).

**Generic remedy — close the claim/operation gap.** Either:

1. *Lower the claim* to match operating reality. Explicit
   acknowledgement that a system runs at rung 1 (e.g. a
   well-functioning religious community honest about its rung-1
   transmission of tradition) is more legitimate than rung-1
   operation under rung-6 cover.
2. *Raise the operation* to match the claim. Citizens' assemblies
   to convert nominal rung-6 democracy into actual rung-6
   deliberation; pre-registration to convert nominal rung-3
   science into actual rung-3 falsification.

Of the two, lowering the claim is rare because high claims confer
near-term legitimacy; raising the operation is the structural fix
the catalogued remedies pursue.

## Pattern C — Cross-Loop Rung Imposition

**Shape.** A controller that operates legitimately at one rung
inside its own loop attempts to extend that rung's authority into
adjacent loops where a different rung is appropriate. The clearest
example is rung-1 religious authority claiming jurisdiction over
rung-3 scientific questions or rung-6 democratic decisions.

**Why it is dangerous.** The encroaching rung is unfit for the
adjacent domain — rung 1 cannot adjudicate empirical questions; rung
3 cannot adjudicate normative questions; rung 6 cannot replace rung 3
in answering "is this drug safe?". The receiving loop loses the
correctional power its rung provides.

**Instances observed.**

- Religious courts ruling on questions of medical fact.
- Scientific authorities ruling on questions of distributive
  justice.
- Constitutional courts ruling on questions that require sustained
  empirical investigation.
- Markets pricing questions whose decisive considerations lie at
  rung 6 (intergenerational duties, rights of the unrepresented).

**Generic remedy — domain-appropriate rung containment.** Each loop's
rung should be set by the question it answers, not by the
institutional power of any controller. Architectural moves:

- Subsidiarity rules that route questions to the rung-appropriate
  forum.
- Standing of independent expert bodies on rung-3/4 questions
  inside otherwise rung-1 institutions (e.g. magisterium-internal
  scientific commissions).
- Judicial restraint doctrines that recognise where a court should
  defer to rung-6 democratic processes.

## Why these three

Each pattern corresponds to one structural relation between the
two ends of a control loop:

| Pattern | Mismatch type |
|---------|---------------|
| A — Asymmetric Loop | Different rungs on the two arrows of the same loop |
| B — Claimed-Rung Inflation | Different claimed and operating rungs at the same controller |
| C — Cross-Loop Rung Imposition | One loop's rung imposed on a loop where a different rung is appropriate |

Pattern A is the most common in hierarchical institutional systems
(religion, military, single-party state). Pattern B is the most
common in nominally-democratic systems (one-party states, captured
universities, captured corporations). Pattern C is the most common
in inter-institutional conflict (church-state, science-policy,
markets-society).

A complete control-structure review for a social system should
explicitly check for all three.
