# Boundaries and Environment

Extracted from `projects/systems-introduction-book/docs/part1/what-is-a-system.md`
(the boundary-question thread running through the critical assessment
of the structural theory).

## The boundary decision is the first analytical act

The most important definitional act for any system is drawing its
boundary: what is inside and what is outside. For social systems
this is particularly fraught:

- Who is "in" a state? Citizens only? Residents? Children? Diaspora?
- Who counts as a member of a religion — the registered faithful,
  the active practitioner, the nominal cultural adherent?
- Where does a corporation end and its supply chain begin?
- Is a family just parents and children, or does it include
  grandparents, cousins, in-laws?

The boundary choice determines:

1. **What counts as a part.** Only things inside the boundary show
   up in the P-level of a decomposition.
2. **What counts as an internal connection.** Relationships between
   insiders are part of the logical architecture; relationships with
   outsiders are interfaces with the environment.
3. **What counts as an external relationship.** Tax collection from
   citizens is internal to a state; tax treaties with other states
   are part of the environment.

A theory of systems that omits the boundary is like a theory of
cells that omits the membrane.

## Where boundaries come from

Boundaries are not given by the system itself; they are drawn by the
analyst as part of the modelling decision. Different boundaries
produce different — but each internally consistent — decompositions.
What matters is:

- The boundary is made **explicit** before the decomposition begins.
- The boundary is **stable** across the five SE levels. A boundary
  that shifts between G, R, F, L, and P breaks the traceability that
  the hierarchy is supposed to provide.
- The boundary is **appropriate to the question being asked**. If you
  are analysing a church's pastoral care function, the boundary may
  be a single parish. If you are analysing its doctrinal governance,
  the boundary may be the entire global hierarchy.

## Boundary vs environment

Once the boundary is drawn, everything outside it is the
**environment** — the set of things the system interacts with but
does not contain. Environment elements show up as:

- **Inputs** the system receives (resources, information, members).
- **Outputs** the system produces (services, waste, influence).
- **Constraints** the environment imposes (laws, markets, weather).
- **Other systems** the system competes or cooperates with.

For social systems the environment usually contains *other social
systems*. A Verein exists within a legal environment (state), a
cultural environment (norms, traditions), and an economic environment
(donations, dues). Analysing only the Verein misses the environmental
forces that shape its behaviour; analysing only the environment
misses the Verein's internal structure. The SE hierarchy makes the
boundary explicit and the environment analysable as external
interfaces at the L and P levels.

## Nested systems and the recursion boundary

Every part of a system is itself a system with its own parts,
connections, and goals. This recursion is not a defect — it reflects
the genuine nested structure of complex systems — but it means every
SE decomposition needs a **recursion boundary**: a decision about
where to stop decomposing. The stop decision is not given by the
theory; it is a modelling choice driven by the question being asked.

A typical project therefore carries two kinds of boundary: the
*outer* boundary (what is in the system being analysed) and the
*recursion* boundary (how far down to decompose its parts). Both
should be stated explicitly at the start of any analysis.
