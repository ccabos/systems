# References

The seven-rung ladder is a synthesis. No single primary source
defines all seven rungs together; the rungs are drawn from several
overlapping traditions in epistemology, philosophy of science, and
political theory.

## On the lower rungs (0–1)

- Weber, M. (1922). *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft.* The classical
  tripartition of authority — charismatic, traditional, legal-rational
  — supplies most of rung 1's content.
- Arendt, H. (1970). *On Violence.* On the distinction between
  power, authority, and force; clarifies why rung 0 (coercion) and
  rung 1 (authority) are different even when they produce
  superficially similar compliance.

## On rungs 2–4 (formal, empirical, cumulative)

- Popper, K. (1959). *The Logic of Scientific Discovery.* The
  classical statement of rung 3 — falsifiability as the demarcation
  criterion.
- Kuhn, T. (1962). *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions.* The
  social side of rung 3 / rung 4 — how a community of inquirers
  collectively maintains and revises the standard.
- Lakatos, I. (1970). *Falsification and the Methodology of
  Scientific Research Programmes.* The progression from rung 3 to
  rung 4 — research programmes as the unit at which empirical
  testing and consilience operate.
- Cochrane Collaboration handbooks. Operational rung 4 — what
  cumulative evidence looks like in evidence-based medicine.

## On rung 5 (meta-rational integration)

- Knight, F. (1921). *Risk, Uncertainty, and Profit.* The
  distinction between risk (calculable) and uncertainty
  (incalculable) is the ground floor of rung 5.
- Funtowicz, S. & Ravetz, J. (1993). "Science for the Post-Normal
  Age." *Futures*, 25(7), 739–755. The explicit argument that some
  policy questions exceed any single method and require
  meta-method reasoning.
- Tetlock, P. & Gardner, D. (2015). *Superforecasting.* Empirical
  evidence on what rung-5 reasoning looks like in practice and why
  it is rare.

## On rung 6 (deliberative legitimacy)

- Habermas, J. (1981). *Theorie des kommunikativen Handelns.* The
  canonical statement of rung 6 — the conditions under which a
  claim is normatively legitimate.
- Rawls, J. (1971). *A Theory of Justice.* The veil-of-ignorance
  procedure as a rung-6 thought experiment.
- Fishkin, J. (2009). *When the People Speak.* The empirical
  literature on deliberative polls and citizens' assemblies — what
  rung 6 looks like operationally.
- O'Flynn, I. (2018). *Deliberative Democracy.* Survey of
  rung-6 institutional design.

## On the technique's STPA hosts

- Leveson, N.G. (2012). *Engineering a Safer World: Systems
  Thinking Applied to Safety.* MIT Press. The canonical STPA
  treatment.
- Leveson, N.G. & Thomas, J.P. (2018). *STPA Handbook.* MIT
  Partnership for Systems Approaches to Safety and Security.

## On the synthesis

The specific seven-rung formulation used here is project-internal.
It is consistent with each of the sources above but is not present
in any one of them. Its development was driven by the observation,
recurrent across the project's social-systems catalogue, that
control-loop failures in those systems are predominantly
*epistemic* — about which standard the receiver is willing to
register a signal at — rather than purely *informational* in the
sense Leveson's safety-engineering examples illustrate.
