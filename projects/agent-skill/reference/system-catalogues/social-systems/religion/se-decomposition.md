# Religion — SE Decomposition

The canonical five-level SE decomposition of religion as a social
system. Extracted from the original decomposition in
`projects/systems-introduction-book/docs/part2/religion.md`, which was
the founding worked example of the project.

For the STPA analysis built on this decomposition (Steps 1–4,
unsafe control actions, loss scenarios, and rung-tagged control
structure) see `applied-se-analysis.md` in this folder.

## Goals (G)

- **G1** — Provide existential meaning and purpose to human life
- **G2** — Establish moral and ethical orientation for individuals and communities
- **G3** — Create social cohesion and collective identity
- **G4** — Offer psychological comfort in the face of suffering and mortality

## Requirements (R)

- **R1** — Coherent cosmological narrative (creation, afterlife, transcendence)
- **R2** — Framework for interpreting personal experience as meaningful
- **R3** — Codified behavioral norms (commandments, precepts, dharma)
- **R4** — Authority structure to interpret and enforce norms
- **R5** — Shared symbols, narratives, and practices for group bonding
- **R6** — Mechanism for distinguishing in-group from out-group
- **R7** — Rituals addressing grief, loss, and life transitions

## Functions (F)

- **F1** — Mythological narration — transmit origin and eschatological stories
- **F2** — Pastoral care — counsel individuals through crisis and life stages
- **F3** — Moral legislation — define permissible and forbidden behavior
- **F4** — Doctrinal governance — interpret scripture, settle disputes
- **F5** — Ritual performance — enact shared ceremonies (prayer, sacraments, festivals)
- **F6** — Missionary expansion — propagate belief system to new populations

## Logical Architecture (L)

- **L1** — Liturgical Subsystem — scripture reading, preaching, ceremonial calendar
- **L2** — Pastoral Subsystem — counseling, rites of passage, community support
- **L3** — Doctrinal/Juridical Subsystem — theology, canon law, ethics boards
- **L4** — Outreach Subsystem — evangelization, education, charity, media

## Physical Implementation (P)

- **P1** — Churches, mosques, temples, synagogues, gurdwaras
- **P2** — Sacred texts — Bible, Quran, Torah, Vedas, Tripitaka
- **P3** — Clergy — priests, imams, rabbis, pastors, monks, gurus
- **P4** — Parish networks, sanghas, congregations, umma structures
- **P5** — Central authorities — Vatican, Al-Azhar, ecumenical councils
- **P6** — Legal systems — canon law, fatwa apparatus, halakhic courts, Sharia boards
- **P7** — Missionary organizations, madrasas, Sunday schools, yeshivas
- **P8** — Religious media — TV networks, podcasts, publishing houses

## Key Structural Observations

**R4 (authority) is the most consequential requirement.** It enables
F3 (moral legislation) and F4 (doctrinal governance), which together
determine how the system interprets and enforces its own norms. When
R4 becomes an end in itself — authority for authority's sake — the
system begins to produce outcomes opposite to its goals. This dynamic
is analyzed in the STPA work under `applied-se-analysis.md`.

**R6 (in-group/out-group) is a double-edged mechanism.** It serves G3
(social cohesion) but contains no built-in limit. The same mechanism
that creates belonging can escalate to dehumanization. The absence of
a structural "circuit breaker" is a key hazard identified in the STPA.

**The Liturgical and Pastoral subsystems (L1, L2) serve different
goals** that can conflict. L1 (liturgy) serves G1 (meaning) and G3
(cohesion) through shared ritual. L2 (pastoral care) serves G4
(comfort) through individual attention. When institutional resources
are scarce, L1 tends to win — communal performance is more visible
than individual care.
