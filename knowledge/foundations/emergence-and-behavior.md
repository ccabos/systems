# Emergence and Behaviour

Extracted from `projects/systems-introduction-book/docs/part1/what-is-a-system.md`
(Theory II — Behaviour). The narrative version with full critical
assessment lives in the book; this file contains only the definitions
and distinctions used by the rest of the project.

## Emergence as the outside-view criterion

A system can be characterised by what it *does* that its parts cannot.
A system exhibits **emergent properties** — properties present in the
organised whole but absent from any of the individual parts considered
in isolation.

No individual neuron is conscious. No individual water molecule is
wet. No individual trader sets market prices. Consciousness, wetness,
and market prices are real; they exist; but they exist only at the
level of the organised system, not at the level of its parts.

This provides an **observational test**:

> Can the behaviour of the whole be predicted by summing the
> behaviours of the parts?
>
> - **Yes** → aggregate.
> - **No** → system.

## Why emergence is not sufficient as a definition

Emergence captures something real and important, but it has three
limitations that prevent it from serving as the *definition* of
"system" on its own. They are preserved here because they recur as
caveats in later analyses.

**1. Emergence is descriptive, not constitutive.** The emergence
criterion tells you what to observe *once you have already decided*
you are looking at a system; it does not tell you how to identify
one. You cannot look for emergence before you have decided what to
treat as the parts — and that decision already presupposes a
structural theory. Emergence is the *output* of a system's structure,
not the criterion that defines it. For SE-based work this is a
practical limitation: you design structure and observe emergence as
a consequence; you cannot design emergence directly.

**2. The criterion is trivially satisfied by aggregates.** A pile of
sand has a height and an angle of repose that no individual grain
possesses. A crowd has a density that no individual person has. These
are properties present in the aggregate but absent from the parts —
but no one seriously claims a pile of sand is a system in the
relevant sense. The emergence criterion must be qualified: the
relevant emergent properties are goal-directed or self-maintaining
behaviours. Once that qualification is added, "goal" is smuggled back
in, and Theory I (structure) is re-introduced.

**3. Strong vs weak emergence.** Philosophers distinguish *weak
emergence* (in principle reducible to lower-level description, but
practically too complex to compute) from *strong emergence*
(genuinely irreducible — a new causal power not explainable even in
principle from the parts). Almost all emergence in social systems is
weak: complex, non-linear, and surprising, but not metaphysically
mysterious. Treating social emergence as *strong* emergence — as if
"the system" were a force beyond analysis — is an error that
forecloses the very analysis this project attempts.

## Use in this project

- The **working definition** (see `system-definition.md`) combines
  structure and emergent behaviour explicitly: "a bounded structure of
  parts and connections organised by goals, whose behaviour at the
  boundary cannot be predicted from its parts in isolation."
- In **STPA** (see `knowledge/se-techniques/stpa/`), the outside-view
  criterion shows up as the requirement that hazards be defined at
  the system boundary, not at the component level.
- In **cross-system comparisons**, emergent behaviours — not shared
  parts — are what make two otherwise different systems (a kingdom
  and a republic, a church and a corporation) recognisable as
  instances of the same abstract type.
