## Stakeholder

Who is asking the question, and in what capacity?

## Scope and boundary

What is inside the system under analysis, and what is outside?
(See `knowledge/foundations/boundaries-and-environment.md` for the
boundary technique.)

## The presenting question

One sentence: what does the stakeholder want to know or change?

## Observed symptoms

What is actually going wrong (or what outcome is not being achieved),
with enough detail that a reader unfamiliar with the situation can
picture it.

## Goals in tension

If the stakeholder has multiple goals, which ones are in tension?
Name the declared goals and — separately — the operative goals
(see `knowledge/foundations/system-definition.md`).

## Constraints

What cannot be changed (legal, contractual, political, physical)?
Constraints are as important as goals — they determine which
remedies are available.

## What has been tried

Previous attempts, their results, and why they did or did not work.
This prevents re-running failed experiments.

## Analytical approach

Which techniques from `knowledge/se-techniques/` will be applied,
and in what order. Typical sequence: SE decomposition → product-line
positioning → STPA or control-structure analysis → remedy proposals.

## Deliverables

What will the stakeholder receive at the end of the engagement? A
decomposition? A set of remedy options? A decision memo? Be
specific — the form of the deliverable shapes how the analysis is
written up.
