# Control Structures in Social Systems

## The Universal Control Pattern

Every social system has a hierarchical control structure with the same basic shape. Authority flows downward; feedback — ideally — flows upward. STPA analyses this structure by asking four questions of every system:

1. **Feedback richness** — how many independent channels carry information from the base to the top, and how accurately do they represent reality?
2. **Self-sealing tendency** — can the process model at the top be corrected by information from below, or does it filter out contradictory signals?
3. **Accountability voids** — where is the controller also the interested party, structurally preventing independent oversight?
4. **Circuit breakers** — what mechanisms exist to interrupt a harmful escalating loop before it causes irreversible damage?

The four levels of the universal pattern are:

| Level | Role |
|-------|------|
| **Supreme Authority** | Issues doctrine, law, strategy, or policy |
| **Intermediate Controllers** | Interpret and transmit downward; aggregate and filter upward |
| **Local Community** | Enforces norms through social mechanisms; primary source of ground-truth feedback |
| **Individual** | Internalises norms; self-regulates; ultimate recipient of system outcomes |

What varies across systems is: who occupies each level, what mechanisms connect them, and how honest the upward flow is.

## The Ten Systems: Control Structure Profiles

### Kingdom (Traditional Monarchy)

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Monarch — authority by hereditary right or divine mandate |
| Intermediate Controllers | Nobility, regional governors, church hierarchy |
| Local Community | Parish, guild, village council |
| Individual | Subject |
| **Feedback richness** | Very low. Upward channels filtered through nobility, who have strong incentives to present the monarch with flattering information. No independent press; no elections. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | High. The monarch's process model is populated almost entirely by the court. External reality reaches the apex only when it becomes impossible to conceal. |
| **Accountability void** | The monarch adjudicates disputes in which the Crown is the interested party. No independent judiciary with power to constrain royal action. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Few and informal: Magna Carta-type bargaining, noble revolt, church censure. All require extreme conditions to activate. |

### Republic (Modern Democracy)

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Constitution and Constitutional Court — above elected government |
| Intermediate Controllers | Elected legislature and executive; civil service |
| Local Community | Municipalities, civil society, free press |
| Individual | Citizen |
| **Feedback richness** | Highest of all systems. Elections, independent press, constitutional courts, ombudspersons, freedom of information, civil society — multiple independent channels, none controlled by the same actor. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Low when institutions are independent. Increases significantly when ruling parties capture judiciary, broadcast media, or electoral administration. |
| **Accountability void** | Emerges when the ruling party controls the institution that reviews its own actions. The structural risk is not the existence of elections but the capture of the institutions that make elections meaningful. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Constitutional review, judicial independence, press freedom, periodic elections, right to petition and protest. The most structurally robust of all systems — deliberately over-engineered with redundant breakers. |

### Theocracy

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Clerical authority claiming direct divine mandate |
| Intermediate Controllers | Regional and local clergy |
| Local Community | Congregation or parish |
| Individual | Believer / citizen (roles merged) |
| **Feedback richness** | Very low. Upward feedback is doctrinal: only information that fits the doctrinal frame is transmitted. Dissent is heresy; empirical contradiction is blasphemy. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Extreme. The supreme authority's process model is explicitly immune to secular challenge by design — divine mandate cannot be falsified. |
| **Accountability void** | Theological authority is simultaneously the rule-maker, the judge, and the enforcer. Civil courts, where they exist, are subordinate to religious law. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Virtually none internal. External circuit breakers (secular courts, international pressure) exist but are structurally excluded from the system. |

### One-Party State

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Party leadership |
| Intermediate Controllers | Party cadre at provincial, district, and workplace level |
| Local Community | Work unit, neighbourhood committee, party cell |
| Individual | Citizen (party membership conditional) |
| **Feedback richness** | Near zero for accurate upward information. Internal party channels exist but carry strong incentives to report compliance rather than problems. Cadre advancement depends on the appearance of success. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Extreme. The party investigates itself, reports on itself, and censors information that contradicts its narrative. The gap between the leadership's process model and operational reality can become catastrophic before it is corrected — as in the Great Leap Forward famine, where local cadres reported record harvests while millions starved. |
| **Accountability void** | Total. Party cadre are investigated by the party; courts are party organs; press is party-controlled. No external oversight with genuine authority. |
| **Circuit breakers** | None structural. Internal coups or leadership succession sometimes function as informal resets, but these are not controllable mechanisms — they are failures of control. |

### Corporation (For-Profit)

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Board of Directors (acting for shareholders) |
| Intermediate Controllers | Executive management (CEO, C-suite) |
| Local Community | Departments, teams, suppliers |
| Individual | Employee / customer |
| **Feedback richness** | Moderate. Financial markets provide quarterly signals; employee turnover signals HR problems; customer churn signals product failures. But all three are lagging, aggregated, and easily manipulated in the short term. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Moderate. External audit theoretically prevents self-sealing at the financial level; in practice, auditor dependence on client revenue recreates the vulnerability (see [Architectural Remedies](remedies.md)). |
| **Accountability void** | Executive compensation is set by board members often appointed on executive recommendation. The audit committee reviews the accounts of the management that appointed its members. Customer harm often only surfaces through litigation, years after the decision. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Regulatory oversight, competition (customers can leave), litigation, press investigation. Stronger than governance-only systems but weaker than the democracy's redundant institutional design. |

### University

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Governing Board / Senate |
| Intermediate Controllers | Senior academics, Dean hierarchy, administration |
| Local Community | Departments, research groups |
| Individual | Researcher / student |
| **Feedback richness** | Moderate. Peer review provides scientific quality feedback; student satisfaction surveys provide teaching feedback; research citation rates provide impact feedback. All three are gameable and have documented failure modes. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Moderate. Peer review by the same community that produced the work creates subtle self-sealing — heterodox ideas face higher rejection rates; established paradigms are resistant to challenge. The replication crisis is the empirical evidence of this. |
| **Accountability void** | Peer review is conducted by potential competitors. Research misconduct is often investigated by the same department that the accused researcher leads. Promotion decisions are made by the same committee whose members the candidate has collaborated with or competed against. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Replication studies, freedom of information for public-funded research, research integrity offices, post-publication peer review. All are recent additions that partially address the self-sealing problem. |

### Military

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | High Command (nominally subordinate to civilian government) |
| Intermediate Controllers | Officers at successive levels of command |
| Local Community | Units, bases, formations |
| Individual | Soldier |
| **Feedback richness** | Low for accurate upward information. The chain of command is also the information channel — the same structure that issues orders filters the reports of their execution. Classification and need-to-know further restrict lateral information flow. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | High. Operational failures are classified; subordinates who report bad news risk their careers; after-action reviews are internal. The process model at the top is populated by the same hierarchy whose performance it should assess. |
| **Accountability void** | Military justice traditionally administered by commanding officers. The commanding officer is simultaneously the authority empowered to suppress investigation and the party whose reputation is affected by its outcome. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Parliamentary oversight (where it exists with real teeth), inspector-general systems, independent military justice. The German Bundeswehr model (mandatory Bundestag vote, independent Wehrbeauftragter) is the most structurally robust implementation. |

### Religion (Church)

The full control structure analysis is in the [STPA analysis of religion](religion-stpa.md). In brief:

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| **Feedback richness** | Very low. Pastoral feedback is filtered through the same clergy who are subject to doctrinal discipline. Lay voice is informal and easily dismissed. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Very high. Doctrine claims divine authority; challenge is redefined as spiritual failure. |
| **Accountability void** | Bishops investigate clergy; clergy investigate lay complaints; the institutional interest in reputation systematically outweighs individual welfare in the control calculus. |
| **Circuit breakers** | External secular law (increasingly), mandatory reporting requirements, lay oversight bodies (recent, partial). |

### Family

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | Parent(s) |
| Intermediate Controllers | (None in nuclear family — flat structure) |
| Local Community | Siblings, extended family, peer group |
| Individual | Child |
| **Feedback richness** | Highly variable. In healthy families: very rich — direct communication, emotional attunement, negotiation. In dysfunctional families: blocked almost entirely — authority without feedback loops. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | High in dysfunctional configurations. Privacy norms ("family matters stay in the family") structurally prevent external correction. |
| **Accountability void** | Parents are both decision-makers and the parties whose behaviour may require oversight. The privacy norm that protects healthy family autonomy simultaneously protects harmful parental conduct from external scrutiny. |
| **Circuit breakers** | Extended family, school, social services, children's ombudsperson institutions. The Nordic children's rights model (dedicated ombudspersons with statutory authority, mandatory reporting by professionals) is the strongest structural implementation. |

### Verein (Voluntary Association)

| Element | Instantiation |
|---------|---------------|
| Supreme Authority | General Assembly — all members |
| Intermediate Controllers | Elected committee (Vorstand) |
| Local Community | Working groups, sections |
| Individual | Member |
| **Feedback richness** | Highest of all ten systems relative to its size. All members have formal voice at the General Assembly; committee decisions are publicly accountable to the membership. |
| **Self-sealing tendency** | Low structurally, but moderate in practice. Active participation rates are typically low, allowing a small engaged group to capture the agenda — a soft form of self-sealing through structural apathy. |
| **Accountability void** | Committee elections can be captured by a self-perpetuating group if membership engagement is low. Annual accounts must be independently audited above certain sizes (German law). |
| **Circuit breakers** | Annual general meeting, right of individual members to call extraordinary meetings, audit requirements, public registry of associations with filing obligations. |

## Cross-System Comparison

| System | Feedback richness | Self-sealing tendency | Accountability voids | Circuit breakers |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Kingdom | Very low | High | Monarch as judge | Few, informal |
| Republic | Very high | Low (when healthy) | Ruling party captures judiciary | Many, redundant |
| Theocracy | Very low | Extreme | Doctrine immune to challenge | None internal |
| One-Party State | Near zero | Extreme | Party self-investigates | None structural |
| Corporation | Moderate | Moderate | Board/exec mutual appointment | Markets, regulation |
| University | Moderate | Moderate | Peer reviews competitor | Replication, integrity offices |
| Military | Low | High | Command investigates conduct | Parliamentary oversight (partial) |
| Religion | Very low | Very high | Hierarchy investigates own abuse | Secular law (recent) |
| Family | Variable | High (privacy norm) | Parents self-oversee | Social services, schools |
| Verein | High | Low | Committee capture risk | General Assembly, audit |

## The Three Most Dangerous Structural Patterns

Across all systems, three patterns recur wherever the most serious harm occurs:

**Pattern 1: The Accountability Void**
The controller and the interested party are the same entity. No external actor has authority to review the decision. This pattern appears in: monarchs judging the Crown's own claims; bishops investigating abuse by their own clergy; commanding officers prosecuting conduct reflecting on their commands; boards setting the pay of executives who helped appoint them. The remedy in every case is the same: structurally separate the controller from the interested party, and give the separating body genuine authority.

**Pattern 2: The Self-Sealing Process Model**
The top of the control structure populates its own process model — its internal representation of reality — from sources it controls. Contradictory information is excluded by design (doctrine), by incentive (career risk for bearers of bad news), or by structure (classification, privacy norms). The model diverges from reality while the authority at the top acts with increasing confidence. The remedy: establish independent information channels with direct access to the apex, reporting to a body outside the hierarchy.

**Pattern 3: The Proxy Metric**
The system measures something that is correlated with its actual goal, attaches resources and careers to that measure, and then watches as the system reorients toward producing the measure rather than the goal. Exam scores replace education. Quarterly earnings replace sustainable value creation. Publication counts replace scientific knowledge. Compliance reports replace operational readiness. The remedy: continuously examine what is being measured and whether the feedback loop connects to the goal or to a proxy, and restructure the measurement accordingly.

These three patterns are the recurring targets of the architectural remedies in this book. Identifying them in a new system is the first step toward designing them out.
